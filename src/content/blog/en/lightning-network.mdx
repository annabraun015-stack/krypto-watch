## The Scalability Trilemma (Definition) <a id="trilemma"></a>

Bitcoin’s base layer (Layer 1) is designed for **Security** and **Decentralization**. However, it is fundamentally slow. With only 7 transactions per second, it cannot handle the daily payments of 8 billion people.

The Lightning Network is the solution. It sits *on top* of Bitcoin, handling millions of small, fast payments while using the massive security of the main blockchain as a "final judge" for any disputes.

---

## What is a Payment Channel? (Technology) <a id="channels"></a>

Imagine a "bar tab" at your local pub.
1.  **Open Tab:** You give the bartender your credit card (On-chain Transaction).
2.  **Order Drinks:** You get 5 beers over 3 hours. No transaction happens yet; the bartender just keeps a running tally (Lightning Transactions).
3.  **Close Tab:** At the end of the night, your card is charged once for the total amount (On-chain Settlement).

In Lightning, these channels are **bilateral smart contracts.**

## How Routing Works: The Onion Model (Network) <a id="routing"></a>

You don't need a direct channel with everyone you want to pay. You can **route** payments through existing nodes.

If Alice has a channel with Bob, and Bob has a channel with Carol, Alice can pay Carol by sending the money *through* Bob. Bob never sees who the money is specifically for; he only sees "receive 100 sats, forward 99 sats." This is called **Onion Routing**, and it makes Lightning significantly more private than the main blockchain.

## Potential Risks and Liquidity Challenges (Security) <a id="risks"></a>

- **Inbound Liquidity:** To *receive* money, your channel must have space to hold it. Managing this "Liquidity" is the biggest hurdle for new users.
- **Watchtowers:** If you go offline for months, your channel partner could theoretically try to "cheat" the close. **Watchtowers** are automated services that monitor the chain for you to prevent this.
- **Forced Closures:** If the network is extremely congested, closing a channel "on-chain" might be expensive.

## Frequently Asked Questions <a id="faqs"></a>

### 1. Does Lightning replace Bitcoin?
No. Lightning **is** Bitcoin. It uses real Bitcoins locked in smart contracts. It is an expansion of Bitcoin's utility, not a separate coin.

### 2. Can I use Lightning on an exchange?
Most major exchanges (like Kraken, Binance, and Coinbase) now support Lightning deposits and withdrawals. This allows you to move funds off the exchange instantly and for nearly zero cost.

### 3. What is an 'Invoice' in Lightning?
Unlike on-chain where you just share an address, in Lightning you usually generate a specific "Invoice" for a specific amount. The sender scans this, and the payment is routed to you instantly.
