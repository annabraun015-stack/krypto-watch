<h2 id="faq">Section 6: FAQ — Truth vs. Fiction</h2>

### 1. Is Bitcoin used mostly for illegal activities?
This is one of the most persistent myths. In reality, Bitcoin is a **terrible** tool for crime because every transaction is recorded on a permanent, public ledger. Chainalysis data shows that illicit use of Bitcoin is consistently under 1%, whereas physical cash remains the primary tool for global money laundering.

### 2. Is Bitcoin bad for the environment?
Bitcoin doesn't "waste" energy; it **consumes** it to secure trillions of dollars of value. In 2026, the mining industry is a leader in renewable energy adoption. By acting as a "Buyer of Last Resort" for stranded energy (like wind or solar in remote areas), Bitcoin actually makes renewable projects more profitable.

### 3. Can Bitcoin be hacked?
The network has been active for over 17 years with 99.98% uptime. To "hack" the ledger, you would need to control more than 51% of the global hash rate—an feat that is physically and economically impossible for any single entity. Individual "hacks" are almost always cases of users losing their [Private Keys](/en/blog/public-private-keys/).

### 4. Is Bitcoin a Ponzi scheme?
A Ponzi scheme relies on secrecy and a central operator. Bitcoin is the opposite: it is **fully transparent** and has no central operator. Everyone can see the code, everyone can audit the supply, and everyone can verify the transactions. It is a neutral commodity, not a financial scheme.

### 5. Why is Bitcoin so volatile?
Volatility is the price we pay for an asset that has **Zero Inflation**. In the legacy system, central banks smooth out volatility by printing more money, which destroys your purchasing power over time. Bitcoin allows the market to set the price purely based on supply and demand, which creates short-term swings but long-term growth.
